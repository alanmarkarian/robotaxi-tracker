Method: everything is per paid mile — empty repositioning/idle miles are baked into each cost line using Waymo's measured ~54–56% paid-mile ratio (bear 50% / bull 65%); 350 operating days/yr. Fares are normalized: every base case uses the same ~$2.60/paid-mile market rate (today's human-driven price), so margin differences come entirely from the cost stack — pricing strategy lives in bear/bull instead. There's no evidence riders pay a premium for a human driver; the measured evidence runs the other way — Waymo commands +12.7% over Uber and riders pay it (Obi, 94k rides). What IS real today: Tesla deliberately prices at half the market ($8.17 avg fare ≈ $1.63/paid mi — the Model Y bear case), and Musk's stated ambition is bus-ticket pricing (the Cybercab bear case). Those are choices about where to set price, not ceilings on what riders would pay. The bear "remote ops" line reflects today's reality — Tesla confirmed to the CPUC that remote operators back every market and has never disclosed the staffing ratio; at tiny fleet sizes it approaches 1:1, which is why the Model Y bear case barely breaks even (independent estimates put Tesla's current all-in cost near $0.81/mi vs Waymo's $1.40). Reference claims: Musk — Cybercab op cost ~$0.20/mi, $0.30–0.40 all-in; ARK — <$0.20/mi by 2030 ("probably true" — Musk) and fares as low as ~$0.25/mi at scale, a volume scenario our per-vehicle bull doesn't assume. Waymo benchmark: 500k rides/wk on ~4,000 cars (~18 rides ≈ ~80 paid mi/car/day — our Model Y bull assumes Tesla nearly doubles that), vehicle cost $75–200k vs Cybercab <$30k, and still not unit-economics positive (Other Bets lost ~$5.5B in 2025) — cheap hardware is Tesla's entire thesis.

Waymo & Uber columns: Waymo revenue anchors to its measured $19.69 avg fare (same Obi study) and measured utilization (500k rides/wk ÷ ~4,000 cars ≈ 18 rides ≈ 80 paid mi/day); its per-vehicle economics are already gross-profitable — the ~$5.5B losses live in R&D and corporate overhead, which no card here includes for any player. Depreciation is its swing line: the bear is today's $150–200k I-PACE fleet, the bull is the ~$85k Zeekr "Ojai". Uber is modeled as a system (rider fare → all costs incl. the driver's own vehicle costs → driver take-home ≈ 55% of fare, Gridwise pay data, Uber take ~28%): the human model spends ~$2.36 to deliver a paid mile that robotaxis deliver for $0.48–1.51 — driver pay (~$1.25/paid mi) is the structural cost the other three models replace with $0.03–0.35 of remote ops. That spread, not the fare, is the whole robotaxi investment thesis.
Key unknowns — what we're watching for as rollout data comes in
  • Remote-operator staffing ratio — the single biggest swing factor; confirmed to exist, ratio never disclosed
  • Tesla's actual deadhead % — we borrow Waymo's CPUC number; Tesla files no AV reports in TX/FL
  • AV fleet insurance actuals — no real per-mile figure exists anywhere yet
  • Cybercab lifetime miles — 500k is an analyst assumption, not a Tesla spec
  • Robotaxi revenue line — buried in "Services & other"; watch for first breakout (Q2 call Jul 22?)
  • 2-seat constraint — 90% of trips carry 1–2 riders (Musk), but airport/group demand leaks to 4-seaters
